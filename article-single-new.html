How To Improve Grades: The Science Behind Sleep


The Biological Clock

Sleep is an altered state of consciousness in which we are less reactive to external stimuli. An average person requires roughly 8 hours of sleep to stay perfectly healthy. It may vary from person to person, as every biological being possesses this biological clock that adjusts to their lifestyle and habits. However, this “Biological clock” is an innate clock that is present in the brain. It dictates our sleep timing based on our regularity and lifestyle. Say, even if you put a person inside a locked room with no windows to know whether it’s day or night, that person would still be able to recognise when it’s time to sleep, as the biological clock would have adapted accordingly.

Hibernation

The biological clock not only affects animals on sleep but also on hibernation. Hibernation might be confused with prolonged sleep, but it is more complicated than that. It might have some similarities, but it creates even more physiological changes than sleeping does. The thing that affects hibernation is temperature. Even if animals do not know what temperature it is outside, they would still remember when it’s their time to hibernate. On a side note, this concept of hibernation includes lowering body temperature (hypothermia), which is used in post-operation heart surgeries so that the heart can slowly repair itself and readjust.


Why we need to sleep

After a workout at the gym, or after an intense study session, We need sleep to regenerate and repair the tissues of our body and build muscle. Sleeping might seem like a waste of time to some of us, but it plays a substantial role in learning and many other things. Ranging from our appearance to our mental state, sleep impacts everything. In fact, after a decent night’s sleep, our skin tends to look younger due to better blood circulation. It repairs UV damage on our skin, thus reducing ageing. The memory circuitry in our brain is also closely related to sleep. Our mind is like a dry sponge. It soaks up information and retains it. But when our body doesn’t get adequate sleep, the memory circuits get clogged. They won’t be able to hold any new information, and the data bounces back. Studies have shown that there is a 40% decrease in the memory retaining capacity when we are sleep deprived. It increases the risk of depression and anxiety and many cardiovascular diseases.


Stages of sleep

There are various stages of sleep. It consists of NREM and REM cycles. 

NREM cycle is further divided into three phases: 

1)Transitional rest is more like the process of transitioning into sleep. During this process, you might experience Hypnic Jerks.

2) The next stage of sleep is Light sleep that goes on for about 10-25 mins. A slow interaction of neurons occurs in the brain, called Sleep Spindles. These are, to simply put, brain waves, which are the electrical pulses that neurons use to communicate with each other and improve the quality of sleep.

3) The third stage, called the Deep Sleep, is the most restorative and vital part of sleep. It determines the quality of your sleep. You often feel groggy and tired when you wake up during this stage. Night terrors and sleep-talking also occur during this stage.


The REM cycle is a dreaming stage. As the name suggests, your eyes start moving rapidly. The brain waves occur even more than in phase 2 and 3, and our body is rendered paralysed. This stage is similar to the first stage of sleep, where we are wakeful.

Waking up amidst this stage causes drowsiness for a long time.


What restores our learning ability?

The sleep spindles which occur during in-depth sleep help in the file-transfer mechanism from a short-term reservoir to a long term storage site. The deeper sleep you get, the better your cognitive abilities become. Sleep also affects the immune system. We know that in response to an antigen(foreign particle), our body produces antibodies to ward off these particles. Similar to these cells, we have Natural Killer Cells, which act as the immune cell’s assassins. They have receptors on them which detect cancerous growth and immediately destroy them. Proper sleep maintains a healthy amount of these NK Cells, without which we would be rendered vulnerable to a myriad of diseases. Sleep also affects our DNA. People who received inadequate sleep in just one night had 711 genes distorted in their body. Half of them had decreased in function, and the other half increased. The half which fell its activity is the Immune Cell genes, whereas the other half, which increased are the cells that promote the growth of tumours, and inflammation, thus increasing the rate of cancer.


So what I wish to convey to you is “Don’t give up on your dreams., Keep sleeping”.
